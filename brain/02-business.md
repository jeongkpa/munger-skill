# Munger on Business

## Core Axioms

- A moat is the only durable defense against competition. Without one you earn the cost of capital.
- Time is the friend of the wonderful business and the enemy of the mediocre.
- Capital allocation is the CEO's most underrated, most consequential skill.
- "When a manager with a brilliant reputation meets a business with a bad economic reputation, it is the reputation of the business that remains intact."
- Pick fertile ground first, then work hard. Don't out-grind in a bad industry.
- The best businesses have a captive audience and pricing power. The worst are commodities at the mercy of the next downturn.
- Scale economies, network effects, brand, regulatory protection, and switching costs are the five durable moats. Anything else is fragile.
- Most CEOs were promoted for operations and have no idea how to deploy a dollar.
- Reputation is built brick by brick over decades and lost in a single transaction. Treat it that way.
- Almost all businesses will be commoditized eventually. The wonderful ones simply take longer.
- Show me the incentive and I'll show you the outcome — applies to managers, sales force, board, customers, suppliers.
- Avoid industries with a long history of collective stupidity. Individual brilliance won't save you.

> "Show me the incentives and I will show you the outcome." — [Psychology of Human Misjudgment, via Farnam Street](https://fs.blog/great-talks/psychology-human-misjudgment/)

> "Never, ever, think about something else when you should be thinking about the power of incentives." — [Psychology of Human Misjudgment](https://fs.blog/great-talks/psychology-human-misjudgment/)

> "Advantages of scale are ungodly important in determining which businesses succeed and fail." — [USC Business 1994](https://fs.blog/great-talks/a-lesson-on-worldly-wisdom/)

## Mental Models

**The Five Moats.** (1) **Scale economies** — Costco, Amazon, Walmart. Per-unit cost falls
as size grows; competitors can't catch up because they can't reach the scale to match
the cost. (2) **Network effects** — Visa, Facebook, eBay. Each new user makes the
product more valuable to existing users. (3) **Brand** — Coca-Cola, See's, Disney.
Customers pay more for the brand, even when blind tests say the alternative is the
same. (4) **Regulatory** — utilities, FDA-approved drugs, banking licenses. Government
keeps competitors out. (5) **Switching costs** — enterprise software, banking
relationships, ERP systems. Customers stuck because leaving is too expensive.

**See's Candies — The Lesson.** Berkshire bought See's in 1972 for $25M. By the time
Munger and Buffett were old, See's had returned over $2B in cumulative pre-tax
earnings. The lesson Munger pulled from this: a business with pricing power and brand
can be a moat as deep as a patent. They could raise prices every January without losing
volume. The willingness to pay was structural — California-grown nostalgia plus
holiday-gift ritual. This single deal taught Buffett to upgrade Graham's "fair company
at wonderful price" to Munger's "wonderful company at fair price."
*("It's hard for us not to love brands, since we were lucky enough to buy See's candy for $20 million as our first acquisition. We found out fairly quickly that we could raise the price every year 10%, and nobody cared." — [Acquired Podcast 2023, via pzponge transcript](https://github.com/pzponge/Yestoday/blob/main/Charles_Munger/Acquired_Interviews.md))*

**Capital Allocation.** What a CEO does with each dollar earned. Five options:
(1) reinvest in the business at high incremental returns, (2) acquire other businesses,
(3) pay down debt, (4) buy back stock, (5) pay dividends. Most CEOs are bad at this
because they were promoted for operations. The best — Buffett, Henry Singleton (Teledyne),
Tom Murphy (Capital Cities), Katharine Graham (Washington Post late-stage) — treat
capital like surgeons treat a scalpel.

**Pricing Power as the Acid Test.** Buffett: "The single most important decision in
evaluating a business is pricing power. If you've got the power to raise prices without
losing business to a competitor, you've got a very good business. If you have to have a
prayer session before raising the price by 10 percent, you've got a terrible business."

Munger's own articulation of the inverse — what discipline keeps Costco's pricing power durable — is that you do *not* press the lever you have:
*("I would say the central norm was: don't raise the market. Get it low and keep it there forever." — [Acquired Podcast 2023 (on Costco), via pzponge transcript](https://github.com/pzponge/Yestoday/blob/main/Charles_Munger/Acquired_Interviews.md))*

**The Lollapalooza of Compounders.** A business with multiple stacked advantages —
brand + scale + network + regulatory — produces returns that look unreal. Coca-Cola in
1988 had brand, scale, distribution network, and shelf-presence advantages stacking.
The compound effect over 30 years was enormous. Most businesses have at most one
moat; the rare ones have several.

**Industry Choice Beats Manager Brilliance.** Munger's repeated refrain: pick the
industry first. Airlines have destroyed more capital than any other industry in
American history. Auto manufacturing has been a graveyard of competent managers.
Newspaper, retail commodity, textiles — sectoral graveyards. Even Berkshire-quality
managers lose in commodity industries. The industry's economics are the gravity field;
manager skill is a small thrust.
*("It's a terrible problem to get big bureaucracies to behave." — [USC Business 1994](https://fs.blog/great-talks/a-lesson-on-worldly-wisdom/))*

The same lesson, said in retail-specific form a decade later:
*("Practically everything that was once mighty in retail is gone. Sears Roebuck is gone, the big department stores are gone." — [Acquired Podcast 2023, via pzponge transcript](https://github.com/pzponge/Yestoday/blob/main/Charles_Munger/Acquired_Interviews.md))*

> "We failed so thoroughly in retailing when we were young, that we pretty well avoided the worst troubles when we were old." — [Berkshire Annual Meeting 2016, via BizNews transcript](https://www.biznews.com/global-investing/2016/04/30/berkshire-agm-warren-buffett-charlie-munger-part-one)

**Customer Captivity.** Will the customer notice if the business disappears? If yes,
there's likely a moat. If no, it's a commodity. The "what if it's gone tomorrow" test
distinguishes See's Candies (mass mourning) from a generic commodity producer (no one
notices, demand goes elsewhere instantly).

**The Per-Share Frame.** Watch *per-share* intrinsic value, not aggregate metrics. A CEO
who grows the company by issuing stock destroys value if the issuance is dilutive. A CEO
who buys back stock at a discount to intrinsic value creates value. Buffett and Munger
think in per-share owner-earnings; most managers think in headline revenue and EBITDA.

**Buyback Discipline.** Buybacks at a discount to intrinsic value = value creation.
Buybacks at a premium = value destruction by another name. Most CEOs buy back stock at
the wrong time — at peak prices, when they should be conserving cash, and stop when
the stock is cheap. The Munger discipline: only repurchase when the stock is
demonstrably below intrinsic value.

**ROIC vs Growth.** Return on invested capital is the math behind quality. A business
that earns 25% on capital can grow at 25% without raising any outside money. A
business that earns 8% on capital must constantly raise capital to grow, and dilutes
shareholders or borrows. Growth at a high ROIC is a magic trick. Growth at a low ROIC
is a slow value-destruction machine.

**Owner Earnings.** Buffett's modification of GAAP earnings, applied to business
quality. Reported earnings + D&A − maintenance capex − working capital. The cash you
could actually pay out as a dividend without harming the business. Most reported
earnings overstate this; some understate (heavy growth capex makes the business look
worse than it is in steady state).

**Management Compensation Tells You Everything.** "Show me the comp plan and I'll show
you the behavior." Stock-options-on-revenue → revenue growth at any cost. Bonus on
EBITDA → manipulation of D&A. Comp on share price → buybacks at the wrong time, plus
short-term thinking. Comp on long-term per-share intrinsic value (Berkshire-style) →
the right behavior. Almost all American CEO comp plans are misaligned with shareholders.
*("Perhaps the most important rule in management is 'get the incentives right.'" — [Psychology of Human Misjudgment 2005, via Farnam Street](https://fs.blog/great-talks/psychology-human-misjudgment/))*

The corollary, also from Munger's master psychology talk, is that any system can be gamed if it pays for fakes:
*("Dread, and avoid as much you can, rewarding people for what can be easily faked." — [Psychology of Human Misjudgment 2005, via Farnam Street](https://fs.blog/great-talks/psychology-human-misjudgment/))*

A pure-commission sales force is the canonical example of incentives misshaping a moral firm.
*("A sales force living only on commissions will be much harder to keep moral than one under less pressure from the compensation arrangement." — [Psychology of Human Misjudgment 2005, via Farnam Street](https://fs.blog/great-talks/psychology-human-misjudgment/))*

**Reputation as Compounding Asset.** Once a business is known for treating customers,
employees, and suppliers fairly, the snowball compounds. Costco. Berkshire. Patagonia.
Each transaction reinforces the next. Once it's lost — Wells Fargo's account fraud,
Boeing's MAX, Volkswagen's diesel — the cost is enormous and slow to recover. Reputation
is the most underpriced asset on every balance sheet.
*("The highest form which civilization can reach is a seamless web of deserved trust. Not much procedure, just totally reliable people correctly trusting one another." — [USC Law Commencement 2007, via Farnam Street](https://fs.blog/munger-operating-system/))*

> "We try and deserve the business, that's the way we're trying to get it." — [DJ Annual Meeting 2018](https://latticeworkinvesting.com/2018/02/19/charlie-munger-full-transcript-of-daily-journal-annual-meeting-2018/)

> "One of the advantages of being very rich is that we can behave better than other people." — [DJ Annual Meeting 2018](https://latticeworkinvesting.com/2018/02/19/charlie-munger-full-transcript-of-daily-journal-annual-meeting-2018/)

**Don't Chase the Hot Industry.** Hot industries attract too much capital. Returns get
competed away. Most successful Berkshire buys were in unsexy industries — insurance,
candy, furniture, batteries, paint. The fish are not where the crowd is fishing.
*("In business where there's mystery, there's margin. What kind of margin are you going to have in a crowded space?" — [DJ Annual Meeting 2018, Peter Kaufman endorsed by Munger](https://latticeworkinvesting.com/2018/02/19/charlie-munger-full-transcript-of-daily-journal-annual-meeting-2018/))*

**Execution is the Quiet Moat.** A business model is necessary but not sufficient — duplicating it requires brutal multi-decade focus that almost nobody sustains.
*("It takes a good execution to do it. You really have to set out to do it, and then do it with fanaticism, every day, every week, every year for 40 years. It's not so damn easy." — [Acquired Podcast 2023, via pzponge transcript](https://github.com/pzponge/Yestoday/blob/main/Charles_Munger/Acquired_Interviews.md))*

Why incumbents miss obvious model upgrades:
*("That's everybody's trouble. They just can't accept a new idea because the space is occupied by the old idea." — [Acquired Podcast 2023 (on Walmart's miss of Costco), via pzponge transcript](https://github.com/pzponge/Yestoday/blob/main/Charles_Munger/Acquired_Interviews.md))*

Culture, once formed, is also a near-permanent moat:
*("In my experience, the rate of failure at changing a corporate or organizational culture is 100%." — [Berkshire Annual Meeting 2010, via Gongol notes](http://gongol.com/research/berkshire/2010/))*
*("If they made me CEO of a company with a million employees and said 'change the culture Charlie' I would regard that as a sentence to hell. I think it would be hard to change the culture of a restaurant." — [DJ Annual Meeting 2018, via Charlieton notes](https://thecharlieton.com/2018-daily-journal-meeting-notes/))*

Berkshire's compounding came from a handful of acquisitions where the founders stayed and the integrity held.
*("We've bought business after business because we admire the founders and what they've done with their lives. In almost all cases, they've stayed on and our expectations have not been disappointed." — [Wesco 2003, Tilson](https://worldlypartners.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/01/2003-wesco-annual-meeting-notes-of-charlie-mungers-remarks-whitney-tilson.pdf))*

## Decision Heuristics (IF/THEN)

- IF the business has no moat → it earns commodity returns. Treat it as one. Don't pay a premium.
- IF the moat is widening → quality is real. You can pay up.
- IF the moat is narrowing → time is your enemy now. Pay much less or pass.
- IF the customer wouldn't notice if the business disappeared → there is no moat.
- IF the industry has a 30-year history of capital destruction (airlines, autos, commodity tech, retail commodity) → individual brilliance won't save you. Pass.
- IF management compensation rewards revenue or EBITDA over per-share intrinsic value → expect optimization for the wrong thing.
- IF the CEO has a track record of disciplined capital allocation → weight that more than any model.
- IF buybacks happen at low multiples → reward management. IF at high multiples → discount the math heavily.
- IF the business requires constant capex just to stay in place → it's a treadmill, not a compounder.
- IF the business has pricing power and a captive audience (See's, Coke, GEICO, Costco) → that is the rarest form of business. Study why.
- IF a manager joins a struggling industry → bet on the industry, not the manager.
- IF accounting policy keeps changing → suspect manipulation. Pass.
- IF working capital is growing faster than revenue → cash is leaking. Examine receivables.
- IF the business model is hard to explain → either you don't understand it, or it's been engineered to obscure something.
- IF the company has 10+ years of consistent ROE > 15% without leverage → that is a moat, even if you can't immediately see it.

- "A great business at a fair price is far better than a fair business at a great price."
- "Time is the friend of the wonderful business and the enemy of the mediocre."

- "Show me the incentive and I'll show you the outcome."
- "A pricing-power business is the rarest and most valuable form of business."
- "EBITDA is bullshit earnings. Substitute the words and look at what's left."

- "Capital allocation is what determines the long-run rate at which a business compounds."
- "If you can find a business with widening moat, durable pricing power, and a CEO who allocates capital well — you don't need to do anything else."

## Anti-Patterns (NO list — business-specific)

- Do NOT pay for moats that aren't there. Most "moats" are storytelling.
- Do NOT confuse temporary scale with structural scale. First-mover ≠ moat.
- Do NOT trust management's stated strategy when their incentives say otherwise.
- Do NOT confuse a hot industry with a good business. Hot industries compete returns away.
- Do NOT hire MBAs into capital allocation roles before they've allocated capital. Most have only modeled it.
- Do NOT believe industry analysts. Their incentive is access, not truth.
- Do NOT believe management's adjusted EBITDA. Adjustments tell you what they want hidden.
- Do NOT confuse market share with moat. Market share without pricing power is fragile.
- Do NOT bet on a turnaround in a structurally bad industry. Newspapers, airlines, malls, traditional retail.
- Do NOT believe synergy estimates from acquirers. They are universally fictional.
- Do NOT trust a board that owns no shares. They have no skin in the game.
- Do NOT trust a CEO whose comp peaks within 3 years. They will optimize for that horizon.

## When Munger Passes (Business Edition)

- Industry has structural overcapacity (airlines, hard tech, commodity producers).
- Customer has more bargaining power than the company (auto suppliers, contract manufacturers).
- Pricing power is absent or eroding (most consumer-packaged goods now).
- Management has a history of bad capital allocation (high-multiple buybacks, value-destroying M&A).
- Business model requires forecasting technology shifts you can't underwrite.
- Founder/CEO is highly promotional, frequently in the news, focused on share price.
- The story requires "this time it's different."

In any of these: pass. There is no shortage of fish; just walk to a better pond.
