# Munger on Character & Life

## Core Axioms

- Avoid envy, resentment, and self-pity at all costs. These three poisons destroy lives.
- Marry the right person. The single highest-leverage decision you will make.
- Be a learning machine. If you go to bed wiser than you woke up, you've had a good day.
- Surround yourself with people better than you, and you will become better yourself.
- Reliability is the most underrated trait. Be someone whose word can be banked.
- Live within your means even when rich. Lifestyle inflation is the silent destroyer.
- Take responsibility for your own life. No one is coming to save you.
- Tell the truth, especially uncomfortable truths. Self-deception ends lives.
- The best way to get a good spouse is to deserve a good spouse.
- "Spend each day trying to be a little wiser than you were when you woke up."
- "Discharge your duties faithfully and well."
- Avoid the Three Cs: Cocaine, Card-tables (gambling), Catastrophic spouses.
- Don't keep score against the neighbors. Inner scorecard only.
- The willingness to bounce back from setbacks is the trait that separates the successful from the broken.

## Mental Models

**The Three Spiritual Diseases.** Munger named envy, resentment, and self-pity as the
three corrosive emotions to avoid above all. Envy is the only deadly sin without any
fun in it. Resentment is drinking poison and hoping the other person dies. Self-pity is
a disease so insidious it dissolves agency. The discipline of refusing to entertain
these three is half the battle of a happy life.
*("Generally speaking, envy, resentment, revenge and self pity are disastrous modes of thought." — [USC Law Commencement 2007, via Farnam Street](https://fs.blog/munger-operating-system/))*

> "Self-pity is not going to improve the situation." — [USC Law Commencement 2007](https://fs.blog/munger-operating-system/)

> "The idea of caring that someone is making money faster [than you are] is one of the deadly sins. Envy is a really stupid sin because it's the only one you could never possibly have any fun at." — [Wesco 2003, Tilson](https://worldlypartners.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/01/2003-wesco-annual-meeting-notes-of-charlie-mungers-remarks-whitney-tilson.pdf)

**Inner Scorecard.** Buffett's frame, fully shared by Munger. Measure yourself by what
you know is true about yourself, not by what others think. The inner scorecard is the
only honest one. Most people live by the outer scorecard — what neighbors think, what
the resume looks like, what the social media reflects — and end up empty regardless of
the wealth.

**Marry the Right Person.** Munger married twice — the first ending in divorce, the
second (Nancy Borthwick) lasting until her death decades later. He repeatedly named
spousal selection as the highest-leverage decision in life. "The best way to get a
good spouse is to deserve a good spouse." Marriage is the multi-decade compounding
machine of all relationships; pick wrong and the compounding works against you.
*("What's the best way to get a good spouse? The best single way is to deserve a good spouse because a good spouse is by definition not nuts." — [Wesco 2003, Tilson](https://worldlypartners.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/01/2003-wesco-annual-meeting-notes-of-charlie-mungers-remarks-whitney-tilson.pdf))*

A more recent and less reverent variant from the Acquired interview, on the variability
of marital outcomes:
*("Half of the marriages in America work pretty damn well. And they would have worked just as well if they were both married to somebody else, by the way." — [Acquired Podcast 2023, via pzponge transcript](https://github.com/pzponge/Yestoday/blob/main/Charles_Munger/Acquired_Interviews.md))*

> "If your proposed marriage contract has 47 pages, my suggestion is do not enter." — [USC Law Commencement 2007](https://fs.blog/munger-operating-system/)

**The Learning Machine.** Munger called himself "a continuous learning machine." He
read several books a week well into his 99th year. The frame: most people stop
learning at 25 and decay from there. The compounders keep learning all their lives,
and at 70 they look unfair compared to their stalled peers. Learning isn't optional
for a good life; it is the infrastructure.
*("In my whole life, I have known no wise people (over a broad subject matter area) who didn't read all the time — none, zero." — [Wesco 2003, Tilson](https://worldlypartners.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/01/2003-wesco-annual-meeting-notes-of-charlie-mungers-remarks-whitney-tilson.pdf))*

> "Without lifetime learning you people are not going to do very well. You are not going to get very far in life based on what you already know." — [USC Law Commencement 2007](https://fs.blog/munger-operating-system/)

> "Acquiring wisdom is a moral duty as well as a practical one." — [USC Law Commencement 2007](https://fs.blog/munger-operating-system/)

> "I am very good at learning things from dead people. That's what everybody should learn." — [CNBC "A Life of Wit and Wisdom" 2023](https://www.cnbc.com/2023/11/30/full-transcript-from-cnbcs-charlie-munger-a-life-of-wit-and-wisdom-.html)

**The Five Closest People.** You become the average of who you spend time with.
Curate ruthlessly. Munger's deliberate move was to live near Buffett and a small
circle of long-term partners — peers who reinforced his thinking and didn't drift to
short-termism. The five-closest-people heuristic: if those five are people who make
you better, you will become better.

**Reliability as Compounding Asset.** Almost everyone is unreliable in small ways. The
person who consistently delivers what they promised becomes irreplaceable in any
group, organization, or partnership. Reliability is rare because it requires saying
no to most things. It compounds because reputation compounds; once you are known as
reliable, opportunities flow to you.
*("If you're unreliable it doesn't matter what your virtues are, you're going to crater immediately." — [USC Law Commencement 2007](https://fs.blog/munger-operating-system/))*

The cheapest filter on the entire enterprise of life is the deserve-it discipline:
*("The best way to avoid envy, recognized by Aristotle, is to plainly deserve the success we get." — [Practical Thought About Practical Thought 1996, via Worldly Partners PDF](https://worldlypartners.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/01/1996-practical-thought-about-practical-thought.pdf))*

A working summary of the lifetime ethos:
*("You want a good reputation when you're all done, not a bad one." — [Acquired Podcast 2023, via pzponge transcript](https://github.com/pzponge/Yestoday/blob/main/Charles_Munger/Acquired_Interviews.md))*

> "The safest way to try and get what you want is to try and deserve what you want." — [USC Law Commencement 2007](https://jamesclear.com/great-speeches/2007-usc-law-school-commencement-address-by-charlie-munger)

**The Reading Habit.** Munger read several hours a day for 70+ years. He claimed he
never met a wise person who didn't read all the time. Books, biographies, science,
history. The discipline of cross-disciplinary reading was the foundation of his
latticework of mental models. "It is the cheapest form of growth and the most
underrated."
*("Learning from other people's mistakes is much more pleasant. The best way to do this is simple: When in doubt, read." — [Munger via 25iq.com (Berkshire annual meeting)](https://25iq.com/2012/12/22/charlie-munger-on-circle-of-competence-the-second-essential-filter/))*

The habit, articulated as a mass observation:
*("I constantly see people rise in life who are not the smartest, sometimes not even the most diligent, but they are learning machines. They go to bed every night a little wiser than they were when they got up and boy does that help, particularly when you have a long run ahead of you." — [Munger via 25iq.com (USC Law 2007)](https://25iq.com/2013/01/27/charlie-munger-on-the-importance-of-worldly-wisdom-and-consistently-not-being-stupid/))*

**The Three Cs (Avoid).** Cocaine. Cards (gambling). Catastrophic spouses. Munger's
half-joking, mostly serious shortlist of the things that ruin most successful men. Some
serious lawyers and bankers he had known had ruined themselves on each. The pattern:
each one captures the decision-making layer, so you cannot rationally evaluate your way
out once you are in.

**Children — Wild Wolves vs. Trained Dogs.** Munger raised eight children, biological
and step. His frame: do not over-protect. Teach by first principles, not commandments.
Let them face real consequences for real decisions while the consequences are still
small. The well-trained dog cannot survive without its trainer; the wild wolf can.

**The Eulogy Method (Life Edition).** Imagine your funeral. Imagine the eulogy. What do
you want said? Now reverse-engineer the life that would deserve it. Most people live
forward and never check; the ones who run this exercise change.

**Live Within Your Means — Even When Rich.** Munger lived in the same Pasadena house
he bought in 1962 until he died. He flew first class only late in life, and only
because Buffett pushed him. The discipline of living below your means even when you
could afford more is what protects you from the lifestyle-trap, where rising spending
makes you a captive of your income.

**The Long Marriage Compounding.** Munger and Nancy: decades of partnership.
Buffett and Susan, then Buffett and Astrid: long-term. Munger's frame: the institution
of marriage compounds in ways no other relationship can, but only if both parties
treat it as a long-term game. Most marriages fail not from incompatibility but from
short-term thinking dressed up as compatibility issues.

**Friendship as Filter.** Choose friends as carefully as you choose spouses. The
friends who make you better are rare. The friends who pull you down are common.
Munger had a small circle of long-term friends and partners — Buffett, the Mungers,
and a handful of other Berkshire-orbit people. He let acquaintances stay acquaintances.

**Reliability and Trust as Multipliers.** A career, a marriage, a partnership, a
network — all run on trust. Trust is built brick by brick over decades. Once lost,
nearly impossible to rebuild. Munger's discipline: never compromise trust for
short-term gain. Almost every cheating he had witnessed in 99 years had cost more in
the long run than it gained in the short.

**The Rule of Equanimity.** Don't react to insults. Don't take offense easily. Don't
keep score against neighbors. Don't celebrate too much when winning or grieve too much
when losing. The composed mind is the productive mind. Munger admired Stoic
philosophers — Seneca, Marcus Aurelius, Epictetus — and lived their advice.

The same mood, in 2017, after a question about how to choose what to do in life:
*("In my whole life I haven't succeeded much at something I wasn't interested in. So I don't think you will succeed if what you're doing all day doesn't interest you." — [DJ Annual Meeting 2017, via VGI Partners notes](https://regalpartnerslics.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/03/February-2017.pdf))*

And in 2018 — at age 94 — the simplest possible recipe for a sustained life:
*("My whole system of life is keeping at it. The task of life is not to see clearly in the distance but to do the task at hand." — [DJ Annual Meeting 2018, via Charlieton notes](https://thecharlieton.com/2018-daily-journal-meeting-notes/))*

**Death as Frame.** Munger lived knowing he would die. He talked about it openly. He
made decisions with the clear-eyed view that the time horizon is finite and the
final ledger is what counts. The frame doesn't make him morbid; it makes him decisive.
Knowing the end clarifies the means.

**Bounce Back as Discipline.** Setbacks happen to everyone. The successful are those
who bounce back. Self-pity is the trap; learning the lesson and moving on is the move.
"Generally speaking, envy, resentment, revenge and self-pity are disastrous modes of
thought. Self-pity gets pretty close to paranoia... Every time you find yourself
drifting into self-pity, regardless of cause, just regard it as a disgraceful condition
and change your behavior."
*("The iron rule of life is everybody struggles." — [CNBC "A Life of Wit and Wisdom" 2023](https://www.cnbc.com/2023/11/30/full-transcript-from-cnbcs-charlie-munger-a-life-of-wit-and-wisdom-.html))*

> "The great philosophers of realism are also the great philosophers of what I call soldiering through. If you soldier through, you can get through almost anything. And it's your only option." — [CNBC "A Life of Wit and Wisdom" 2023](https://www.cnbc.com/2023/11/30/full-transcript-from-cnbcs-charlie-munger-a-life-of-wit-and-wisdom-.html)

> "I basically believe in the soldier on system. Lots of hardship will come and you gotta handle it well by soldiering through. And lots of, a few rare opportunities will come. You got to learn how to recognize them when they come." — [CNBC "A Life of Wit and Wisdom" 2023](https://www.cnbc.com/2023/11/30/full-transcript-from-cnbcs-charlie-munger-a-life-of-wit-and-wisdom-.html)

> "Avoid crazy at all costs. Crazy is way more common than you think. It's easy to slip into crazy. Just avoid it, avoid it, avoid it." — [CNBC "A Life of Wit and Wisdom" 2023](https://www.cnbc.com/2023/11/30/full-transcript-from-cnbcs-charlie-munger-a-life-of-wit-and-wisdom-.html)

**The Discipline of Equanimity Around Status.** People who live by the outer scorecard
are slaves to social comparison. People who live by the inner scorecard are free.
Munger noticed that the wealthiest men he knew were often the unhappiest, because they
had built their identity on outpacing peers — and the peer set keeps recalibrating.
Wealth without an inner scorecard is a treadmill.

## Decision Heuristics (IF/THEN)

- IF you cannot trust someone's integrity → never partner, never marry, never invest. Walk.
- IF you envy someone → you've already lost. Stop and find the wound.
- IF a relationship requires constant managing → reconsider the relationship.
- IF you are not learning every day → you are decaying. Reading is the cheapest form of growth.
- IF a peer group pulls you down → change peer groups. You become the average of who you spend time with.
- IF you've made a mistake → admit it, understand it, move on. Self-pity guarantees repetition.
- IF you cannot stand alone with your own thoughts → fix that before fixing anything external.
- IF you are tempted to live above your means → don't. Lifestyle inflation is the silent killer.
- IF you've found a worthy life partner → close the door behind you.
- IF a friend has a destructive habit → distance yourself. You will become like them or you won't.
- IF you've been promoted to a job that requires lying → leave. The corruption compounds.
- IF you find yourself rationalizing a small ethical breach → it's the start of a bigger one. Stop.
- IF you've gotten unexpectedly rich → behave the same as before. Don't change your friends, your house, or your habits except slowly.
- IF you have children → teach them by first principles, not commandments. Don't over-protect.
- IF you've reached a goal → pause and re-evaluate before chasing the next. Most goals are commitments to dissatisfaction.
- IF a close one is dying → be present. Most regret is about absence.
- IF you are angry → wait 24 hours before acting. Most anger does not survive the night.

## The Iron Prescriptions for a Happy Life (Munger's USC Law Commencement, 2007)

A condensed version of the speech that became the most-quoted Munger life-advice
material outside of investing:

1. **Don't sell anything you wouldn't buy yourself.**
2. **Don't work for anyone you don't respect and admire.**
3. **Work only with people you enjoy.**
4. **Be a learning machine.** Read continuously.
5. **Spend less than you earn, invest the difference, and avoid debt.**
6. **Avoid envy, resentment, and self-pity.**
7. **Choose your spouse and your friends with care; you become the average of them.**
8. **Bounce back from setbacks. The successful are not the ones who never fail; they
   are the ones who keep going.**
9. **Always assume there is a 99% chance you are missing something.**
10. **Maintain personal honesty.** Reputation and integrity are the most valuable
    assets you can build, and they can be lost in an instant.

## Anti-Patterns (NO list — character/life edition)

- Do NOT entertain envy. Name it and starve it.
- Do NOT carry resentment. It poisons the carrier, not the target.
- Do NOT engage in self-pity. It is paranoia in costume.
- Do NOT marry someone you cannot trust. The pain compounds.
- Do NOT compete with neighbors on lifestyle. The treadmill has no exit.
- Do NOT take on consumer debt. It is the slow killer.
- Do NOT skip exercise, sleep, or reading. The body and mind compound or decay.
- Do NOT do business with people of low integrity, regardless of how lucrative.
- Do NOT live above your means. Lifestyle inflation cannot be undone easily.
- Do NOT moralize at others about their lives. They will resist and resent.
- Do NOT become identified with your job, status, or wealth. Each is fragile; identity should be deeper.
- Do NOT keep emotional scorecards. Forgive selfishly — it frees you.
- Do NOT raise heirs to expect free money. It will ruin them.
- Do NOT chase the latest hot ideology. Most are tribal, not true.
- Do NOT mistake busyness for purpose. The busy life is often the unexamined one.

## Munger's Closing Frame on Life

He said it many ways but the throughline is consistent: the good life is the disciplined
life. Discipline of reading, of marriage, of friendship, of money, of mind. The
disciplined life produces happiness as a byproduct. The undisciplined life produces
misery even when wealthy. Most prescriptions for happiness are scams; the real ones
are simple, hard, and old.

Take a simple idea and take it seriously.
*("Spend each day trying to be a little wiser than you were when you woke up. Discharge your duties faithfully and well. Step by step you get ahead, but not necessarily in fast spurts." — [Wesco 2003, Tilson](https://worldlypartners.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/01/2003-wesco-annual-meeting-notes-of-charlie-mungers-remarks-whitney-tilson.pdf))*
