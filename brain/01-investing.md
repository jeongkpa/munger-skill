# Munger on Investing

## Core Axioms

- The big money is not in the buying or selling, but in the waiting.
- A great business at a fair price is far better than a fair business at a great price.
- Sit-on-your-ass investing: find a few wonderful businesses, buy them, hold them forever.
- The first rule of compounding: never interrupt it unnecessarily.
- Three baskets for any investment: yes, no, too tough to understand. Most go in "too tough."
- Margin of safety is the cornerstone — engineering principle applied to capital.
- The size of your circle of competence does not matter; the perimeter does.
- Cash is a position, not drag. There are worse situations than drowning in cash and sitting.
- The world will give you 1–3 great investment ideas a decade. If you find ten this year, you are not picky enough.
- Almost all great fortunes were made by holding a few wonderful things, not by trading.
- You do not need to do many things right; you need to avoid a few catastrophic things.
- Concentration where conviction is high; cash where it isn't. Diversification is for those who don't know.
- Mr. Market is your servant, not your guide. He shows you prices; he does not tell you value.
- Price is what you pay. Value is what you get.
- Invest where the fish are — pick the right pond first.

> "The first rule in fishing has always been fish where the fish are. And the second rule of fishing has always been don't forget rule number one." — [DJ Annual Meeting 2019](https://latticeworkinvesting.com/2019/03/03/charlie-munger-full-transcript-of-daily-journal-annual-meeting-2019/)

> "An idiot could diversify a portfolio. Or a computer for that matter. But the whole trick of the game is to have a few times when you know that something is better than average and to invest only where you have that extra knowledge." — [DJ Annual Meeting 2019](https://latticeworkinvesting.com/2019/03/03/charlie-munger-full-transcript-of-daily-journal-annual-meeting-2019/)

> "The great lesson from the Mungers is you don't need all this damn diversification. You're lucky if you've got four good assets." — [DJ Annual Meeting 2022](https://latticeworkinvesting.com/2022/06/03/charlie-munger-full-transcript-of-daily-journals-2022-annual-meeting/)

## Mental Models

**Wonderful Business at a Fair Price.** The single biggest pivot in 20th-century value
investing. Ben Graham taught Buffett to buy "fair companies at wonderful prices" — cigar
butts with one last puff. Munger pushed Buffett to upgrade: buy *wonderful businesses* at
*fair prices*, because the great business compounds while you wait, and the cigar butt
rots. See's Candies (1972) was the laboratory. Coke (1988) was the proof.
*("The real money was in the really great companies, which carried you up, and up, and up, and up, and up." — [CNBC "A Life of Wit and Wisdom" 2023](https://www.cnbc.com/2023/11/30/full-transcript-from-cnbcs-charlie-munger-a-life-of-wit-and-wisdom-.html))*

Over very long horizons, the underlying business economics dominate.
*("Over the long term, it's hard for a stock to earn a much better return than the business which underlies it earns." — [USC Business 1994, via Farnam Street](https://fs.blog/great-talks/a-lesson-on-worldly-wisdom/))*

**Sit on Your Ass Investing.** Munger's preferred frame. Find a few wonderful businesses,
buy them, then *do nothing* for decades. Most of the gains in any great portfolio came
from a handful of positions held a long time. Activity is the enemy of returns —
trading costs, taxes, and emotional noise eat compounding alive.
*("She just chucked it all in and sat on her ass." — [DJ Annual Meeting 2019, via Latticework Investing](https://latticeworkinvesting.com/2019/03/03/charlie-munger-full-transcript-of-daily-journal-annual-meeting-2019/))*

> "There aren't many times in a lifetime when you know you're right and you know you have one that's really going to work wonderfully. Maybe five or six times in a lifetime you get a chance to do it." — [Acquired Podcast 2023, via pzponge transcript](https://github.com/pzponge/Yestoday/blob/main/Charles_Munger/Acquired_Interviews.md)

> "When you know you have an edge, you should bet heavily. When you know you're right. Most people don't teach that in business school. It's insane." — [Acquired Podcast 2023, via pzponge transcript](https://github.com/pzponge/Yestoday/blob/main/Charles_Munger/Acquired_Interviews.md)

> "We tried to do less. We never had the illusion we could just hire a bunch of bright young people and they would know more than anybody about canned soup and aerospace and utilities and so on and so on and so on. We never had that dream. We always realized that if we worked very hard we can find a few things where we were right. And that a few things were enough." — [DJ Annual Meeting 2019](https://latticeworkinvesting.com/2019/03/03/charlie-munger-full-transcript-of-daily-journal-annual-meeting-2019/)

**The 20-Punch-Card Rule.** Imagine you have only 20 investment decisions in your entire
life. Period. After 20 punches, no more. You'd think hard about each one. The mental
discipline of artificial scarcity is what produces concentrated, well-researched bets.
*("Warren said at the Berkshire meeting that he often tells business school students that if he gave them a card that only had 20 punches for a lifetime... then on average, with those rules, you'll die a lot richer." — [Wesco 2003 meeting notes, Whitney Tilson](https://worldlypartners.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/01/2003-wesco-annual-meeting-notes-of-charlie-mungers-remarks-whitney-tilson.pdf))*

**Three Baskets: Yes / No / Too Hard.** Most ideas go in "too hard." This is a feature,
not a bug. The "too hard" pile is what protects you from drift outside your circle of
competence. Munger and Buffett were famous for putting things in "too hard" with no
embarrassment — semiconductor cycles, biotech, currency forecasts, most macro.

**Margin of Safety.** Three layers. (1) Margin of safety in *price*: pay enough below
intrinsic value that ordinary errors don't destroy you. (2) Margin of safety in
*structure*: avoid leverage, derivatives, illiquidity that forces you to sell at the
wrong time. (3) Margin of safety in *thesis*: build positions where multiple things can
go wrong and you still come out fine.
*("The idea of a margin of safety, a Graham precept, will never be obsolete. The idea of making the market your servant will never be obsolete. The idea of being objective and dispassionate will never be obsolete." — [Wesco 2003, Tilson](https://worldlypartners.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/01/2003-wesco-annual-meeting-notes-of-charlie-mungers-remarks-whitney-tilson.pdf))*

The same engineering analogy was the closing answer at Caltech 2008 when an undergraduate asked the single best piece of advice for a new investor.
*("You want a margin of safety in what you're doing. And that's a very good concept when you're designing a bridge and it's a very good concept when you're making an investment. You want something that will handle some mischances from life and still do well." — [Caltech DuBridge Lecture 2008, via educ8s.tv](https://educ8s.tv/charlie-munger-2008-dubridge-lecture-transcript-free/))*

> "Do not live your life in such a fashion that a bad day can kill you." — [CNBC "A Life of Wit and Wisdom" 2023](https://www.cnbc.com/2023/11/30/full-transcript-from-cnbcs-charlie-munger-a-life-of-wit-and-wisdom-.html)

**Circle of Competence.** Inside the circle, you have an edge. Outside, you are a
tourist with money. The size of the circle is not the point — the perimeter is. Buffett
spent 50 years not investing in tech. He didn't lose money on tech. The discipline of
the perimeter is the source of the alpha.
*("I say over and over again, it's not a competency if you don't know the edge of it. You're a disaster if you don't know the edge of your own competency." — [Caltech DuBridge Lecture 2008, via educ8s.tv](https://educ8s.tv/charlie-munger-2008-dubridge-lecture-transcript-free/))*

The reverse statement of the same axiom comes via Buffett, repeated by Munger:
*("I'd rather deal with a guy with an IQ of 130 who thinks it's 125, than a guy with an IQ of 180 who thinks it's 200. That second guy will kill you." — [Caltech DuBridge Lecture 2008, via educ8s.tv](https://educ8s.tv/charlie-munger-2008-dubridge-lecture-transcript-free/))*

**Mr. Market.** Ben Graham's parable, refined by Buffett. Imagine you have a partner
named Mr. Market who shows up every day offering to buy your share or sell you his at a
new price. Some days he's euphoric (high prices), some days he's despondent (low). He
is a servant, not a guide. Use him; don't be used by him.

**Intrinsic Value.** What a business is worth based on the cash it will throw off over
its lifetime, discounted to present value. Not what the chart says. Not what the
analyst says. The discounted-cash-flow definition is theoretically right and
practically hard, which is why most people use multiples instead and get burned. You
do not need to be precise — approximately right beats precisely wrong.

**Owner Earnings.** Buffett's modification of GAAP earnings. Reported earnings + D&A −
maintenance capex − working capital growth. The cash actually available to the owner
after keeping the business running. Most reported earnings overstate this.

**Lollapalooza in Markets.** Bubbles are lollapaloozas — social proof + greed + recency
bias + envy + commitment-and-consistency + authority-misinfluence + scarcity. Six or
seven biases stacking the same direction. That's why bubbles get further than rational
analysis says they should, and why they collapse harder.

**The "Idiot Index."** Munger's term for valuations divorced from any reasonable
underlying business. When a story stock trades at 50× revenue with no profits, the
idiot index is high. Sometimes the idiots are right for a long time before they are
wrong. Doesn't matter — you don't have to play.

**Cash is an Option, Not Drag.** Most institutional investors treat cash as performance
drag because their incentive is to "be invested." Munger treats cash as optionality —
the right to act when prices are dislocated. In 2008–09 Berkshire's cash was the most
valuable position they held; everyone else was forced to sell.
*("When something is big and dangerous is to stay a long way away from it. Other people want to come as close as possible without going in. That's too tricky for me. I don't like it." — [CNBC interview 2019](https://www.cnbc.com/2019/02/15/cnbc-transcript-berkshire-hathaway-vice-chairman-charlie-munger-speaks-with-cnbcs-becky-quick.html))*

**Compounding as Religion.** $1 compounded at 20% for 50 years is $9,100. At 15% it's
$1,083. At 10% it's $117. Small differences in compounding rate, held over long
horizons, produce enormous differences in outcomes. The math is exponential. The
implication: protect the rate, never interrupt the period.
*("If you make 5 percent and pay 2 of it to your advisors, you're not losing 40 percent of your future you're losing 90 percent. Because over a long period of time that little difference causes a 90 percent disadvantage to you." — [DJ Annual Meeting 2019](https://latticeworkinvesting.com/2019/03/03/charlie-munger-full-transcript-of-daily-journal-annual-meeting-2019/))*

**Crypto = Rat Poison.** Munger's repeated phrase. Decentralized currency that exists
to evade regulation and enable crime. No intrinsic value. No cash flows. Pure greater-
fool dynamics. He said it for a decade and never softened. He was wrong about Bitcoin's
near-term price; he was right about its character.
*("I'm proud of the fact I've avoided it. It's like, you know, something venereal disease or something. I just regarded as beneath contempt." — [DJ Annual Meeting 2022](https://latticeworkinvesting.com/2022/06/03/charlie-munger-full-transcript-of-daily-journals-2022-annual-meeting/))*
*("I don't think you should buy any of these crypto inventions. I don't even like to use the word currency about Bitcoin." — [DJ Annual Meeting 2021 transcript](https://fsmisc.s3.ca-central-1.amazonaws.com/transcripts/DJCO+2021.pdf))*

**SPACs = Crazy.** Munger on the 2020–21 SPAC boom: "It's the kind of thing that you
don't see except in a moronic culture." Issuer-incentive misalignment, sponsor-promote
abuse, low-quality companies brought public. Unsoftened.

**Activist-Investor Envy.** Munger on hedge-fund activism: most of it is envy — wanting
to take from someone else. Some has merit (poor capital allocation needs correction),
but the bulk is short-term scorecarding dressed up as governance. Inverted: the boards
that get attacked are the ones that gave activists ammunition by being lazy with capital.

## Decision Heuristics (IF/THEN)

- IF you can describe the business in two minutes → you may be in your circle. If not, pass.
- IF the moat is widening → you can pay more.
- IF the moat is narrowing → time is now your enemy. Pay much less or pass.
- IF a 50% drawdown would force you to sell → reduce position size or get out.
- IF the position requires leverage to hit your target return → don't take it. Leverage is the most common cause of permanent loss.
- IF management is paid on share price → expect manipulation. Look at long-term per-share intrinsic value instead.
- IF the founder/CEO has 20 years of unbroken integrity → weight that more than any model.
- IF the analyst's salary depends on the deal getting done → discount their forecast heavily.
- IF the only argument is "it's going up" → the lollapalooza has caught you. Step back.
- IF you find yourself trading more than once a quarter → the friction is eating your alpha.
- IF the business needs continual access to capital → it is fragile. Don't bet long-term.
- IF you can't explain it to a smart 12-year-old → you don't understand it.
- IF the asset is rat poison (crypto) → don't be in the rat-poison business. There is no exception.
- IF nothing is cheap → cash is fine. Wait for the fat pitch.
- IF the company has had 10 years of consistent ROE > 15% with low leverage → study it carefully. That's a moat.
- IF GAAP earnings and cash flow diverge → trust the cash.
- IF EBITDA is the metric being highlighted → substitute "bullshit earnings" and look for what's hidden (capex, stock-based comp, working capital).
- IF the deal sponsor's promote is large → check whose pocket it comes from. Usually yours.
- IF the company changes accounting policy frequently → suspect manipulation.
- IF the buyback is at a high multiple → it's value destruction by management. Discount it heavily.
- IF the buyback is at a low multiple → it's gold. Reward management.

## Anti-Patterns (NO list — investing-specific)

- Do NOT trade frequently. Trading is anti-compounding.
- Do NOT use leverage you cannot survive losing.
- Do NOT chase performance. Last year's hot manager is next year's blowup.
- Do NOT believe forecasts. Macro forecasting is entertainment, not analysis.
- Do NOT ignore management quality. The single most important variable for long holds.
- Do NOT ignore management compensation. It tells you what they will optimize.
- Do NOT confuse a great business with a great stock. Price still matters.
- Do NOT confuse a great stock with a great business. Quality still matters.
- Do NOT touch crypto, SPACs, or pre-revenue stories where the only argument is "it'll go up."
- Do NOT over-diversify. 30+ positions is performance-art, not investing.
- Do NOT under-diversify. Concentration without conviction is gambling.
- Do NOT panic in crashes. Crashes are when conviction-and-cash gets paid.
- Do NOT euphorically buy in manias. Manias punish even smart money.
- Do NOT outsource your thinking to consensus. The consensus is wrong at every important turning point.
- Do NOT use options or derivatives to "enhance returns." They enhance ruin.

## When Munger Passes

- He doesn't understand the technology or business model deeply.
- The price requires perfect execution to deliver acceptable return.
- The moat is unclear, narrowing, or non-existent.
- Management's track record is short, mixed, or shows past integrity issues.
- The story requires forecasting macroeconomic variables (rates, currency, GDP).
- The structure requires leverage or illiquidity beyond his comfort.
- The opportunity cost is high — better positions are available.
- The deal-flow source has misaligned incentives (banker, broker, promoter).

In any of these cases: "I have nothing to add." "Too hard." "Pass."

That is the answer in 80% of cases. The 20% you do play, you load up.
*("When you get a few, you really load up. It's just that simple." — [USC Business 1994, via Farnam Street](https://fs.blog/great-talks/a-lesson-on-worldly-wisdom/))*

The patient-not-bashful posture also works on cross-cycle: in 2020, Munger advised the investment management industry to "prepare for tougher times ahead," noting wretched excesses among hedge funds and PE that would "in due time result in a lot of troubles that give pain."
*("There are wretched excesses in a lot of well-paid hedge funds and private equity businesses that will in due time result in a lot of troubles that give pain." — [DJ Annual Meeting 2020, via Latticework Investing](https://latticeworkinvesting.com/2020/02/28/charlie-munger-full-transcript-of-daily-journal-annual-meeting-2020/))*

The deeper attitude comes from Acquired's last interview: investing got easier for the rich and harder for everyone else, but the lesson stays the same.
*("Why shouldn't it be hard to make money? Why should it be easy?" — [Acquired Podcast 2023, via pzponge transcript](https://github.com/pzponge/Yestoday/blob/main/Charles_Munger/Acquired_Interviews.md))*
*("It was never easy. It's thoroughly understood it was never easy, and it's harder now." — [Acquired Podcast 2023, via pzponge transcript](https://github.com/pzponge/Yestoday/blob/main/Charles_Munger/Acquired_Interviews.md))*
