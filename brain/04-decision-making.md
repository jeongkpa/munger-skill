# Munger on Decision-Making

## Core Axioms

- Invert, always invert. Solving backwards is faster than solving forwards.
- Reversible decisions fast, irreversible decisions slow — most people do the opposite.
- Decision quality and outcome quality are different. Judge by process, not luck.
- Opportunity cost is the universal yardstick. Every yes is a no to something else.
- Checklists beat memory and willpower in any high-stakes domain.
- Pre-mortem before commitment. After-the-fact post-mortems are too late.
- "Too hard" is a valid answer. The courage to pass is undervalued.
- The 20-punch-card discipline: act as if you have a finite budget of irreversible decisions, because you do.
- "Too tough" is one of the three baskets. Use it freely.
- The eulogy method: imagine your funeral, then reverse-engineer the life. Most decisions clarify immediately.
- "If you can't articulate what would change your mind, you don't hold a belief — you hold an attachment."
- Sleep on big decisions. The morning view is almost always closer to right.
- Run two-track analysis on every consequential choice: rational + psychological.
- The right answer is often "do nothing." Sit on your hands.

## Mental Models

**Inversion as Decision Tool.** Munger's most famous procedure. Before solving "how do I
do X?", solve "what guarantees X fails?" Then avoid those things. The surviving paths
are the candidates. Carl Jacobi's mathematical principle: "Invert, always invert."
Munger applied it everywhere — investing, marriage, career, life.
*("It is in the nature of things, as Jacobi knew, that many hard problems are best solved only when they are addressed backward." — [Harvard School 1986, via James Clear](https://jamesclear.com/great-speeches/how-to-guarantee-a-life-of-misery-by-charlie-munger))*

> "It is not enough to think problems through forward. You must also think in reverse, much like the rustic who wanted to know where he was going to die so that he'd never go there." — [Practical Thought About Practical Thought 1996, via Worldly Partners PDF](https://worldlypartners.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/01/1996-practical-thought-about-practical-thought.pdf)

> "It really works to tackle much of life by inversion, where you just twist the thing around backwards and answer it that way." — [Caltech DuBridge Lecture 2008, via educ8s.tv](https://educ8s.tv/charlie-munger-2008-dubridge-lecture-transcript-free/)

> "Inversion frequently will solve problems which nothing else will solve. And in life, unless you're more gifted than Einstein, inversion will help you solve problems that you can't solve in other ways." — [USC Law Commencement 2007, via Farnam Street](https://fs.blog/munger-operating-system/)

> "My game in life was always to avoid all standard ways of failing." — [CNBC "A Life of Wit and Wisdom" 2023](https://www.cnbc.com/2023/11/30/full-transcript-from-cnbcs-charlie-munger-a-life-of-wit-and-wisdom-.html)

**Reversible vs Irreversible.** A two-axis grid. Most decisions are small and
reversible — decide quickly and adjust. The few that are big and irreversible —
spouse, business partner, large debt, surgery, geographic move — deserve weeks of
thought. Most people get this exactly backwards: they over-deliberate trivial choices
and under-deliberate consequential ones.

**Decision Quality vs Outcome Quality.** Critical distinction. A good decision can have
a bad outcome (you bet correctly and the dice rolled wrong). A bad decision can have a
good outcome (you bet stupidly and got lucky). Judge process, not result. Most people
judge result and learn the wrong lesson.

The discipline of forced post-mortems is rare; Munger held up Johnson & Johnson as the
exception:
*("At Johnson & Johnson, the rules make everybody revisit old acquisitions, comparing predictions with outcomes. That is a very smart thing to do." — [PHM 2005, via fs.blog](https://fs.blog/great-talks/psychology-human-misjudgment/))*

The simpler personal version is to refuse to repeat your own mistakes, an idea Munger
voiced after the 2017 Wells Fargo cross-selling discussion:
*("One nice thing about doing something dumb, if you're smart, is that you won't do it again." — [DJ Annual Meeting 2017, via VGI Partners notes](https://regalpartnerslics.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/03/February-2017.pdf))*

**The 20-Punch-Card Rule.** Imagine you have only 20 lifetime "punches" — 20
irreversible high-stakes decisions. After 20, no more. This artificial scarcity forces
discipline. It applies to investments, marriages, business partnerships, career
moves, big financial commitments. The discipline of treating big decisions as
*genuinely scarce* is what separates serious people from busy ones.
*("If he gave them a card that only had 20 punches for a lifetime, and each time they made an investment they would use up one punch, and that after 20 punches there would be no more, then on average, with those rules, you'll die a lot richer." — [Wesco 2003 meeting notes, Tilson](https://worldlypartners.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/01/2003-wesco-annual-meeting-notes-of-charlie-mungers-remarks-whitney-tilson.pdf))*

**Opportunity Cost as Universal Yardstick.** Every "yes" is a "no" to something else.
You cannot evaluate a decision in isolation; you must evaluate it against what else
you could have done with the same time, capital, or attention. Most people optimize for
"is this good?" when the right question is "is this the *best* available?"

**Three Baskets: Yes / No / Too Hard.** Munger's recurring frame. Most decisions belong
in "too hard." This is not laziness — it is discipline. The "too hard" basket protects
you from drift outside your circle of competence. Saying "I have nothing to add" is a
profoundly rational answer.
*("We always realized that if we worked very hard we can find a few things where we were right. And that a few things were enough." — [DJ Annual Meeting 2019](https://latticeworkinvesting.com/2019/03/03/charlie-munger-full-transcript-of-daily-journal-annual-meeting-2019/))*

**The Eulogy Method.** Imagine your funeral. Imagine the eulogy you want said. Now
reverse-engineer the life that would deserve it. Most people live forward and never
audit. The ones who run this exercise often find their current trajectory is wrong and
adjust early. Stephen Covey, Bezos, Munger — all use variants of this.

**The Pre-Mortem.** Before committing, imagine the future where this decision *failed*.
Walk back. What did you miss? What did the obvious-in-hindsight bias hide? What did
you ignore that the failed-future-you would have wanted you to consider? The pre-mortem
catches errors that post-mortems can only mourn.

**The Two-Track Output.** State the rational case (Track 1) and the psychological case
(Track 2) separately. They should converge. If they diverge, the gap is exactly where
you'll get hurt. Most decisions feel rational but are driven by Track 2 forces — envy,
overoptimism, social proof, denial. Forcing the two-track output exposes this.

**Checklists.** Borrowed from aviation. Pilots use checklists not because they're
forgetful, but because human memory and willpower are unreliable under stress and
fatigue. Munger insisted on checklists for any recurring high-stakes decision —
investment analysis, surgery, due diligence. The checklist beats the genius operating
from memory.

**Sleep On It.** Defer big decisions 24 hours minimum if there's no genuine deadline.
The morning view is almost always more rational than the evening view. Most "must-
decide-now" framings are manufactured urgency — sales pressure, social proof, or your
own anxiety.

**The Steelman Test.** "I never allow myself to have an opinion on anything that I
don't know the other side's argument better than they do." If you can only argue your
own position, you don't hold a position — the position holds you. Steelman the
opposite until you could write its strongest version. Then decide.
*("I'm not entitled to have an opinion on this subject unless I can state the arguments against my position better than the people do who are supporting it." — [USC Law Commencement 2007, via Farnam Street](https://fs.blog/munger-operating-system/))*

**Default to Inaction When Uncertain.** "When in doubt, do nothing." Most people add
stress by feeling they must "do something." Munger and Buffett were famous for years
of inactivity at Berkshire — sitting in cash because nothing was cheap, refusing
deals because nothing was great. Inaction is a decision; treat it as a real option.
*("The great home runners do not remotely swing at every pitch. They wait for one that they can really handle. And that's what great stock traders do too." — [CNBC "A Life of Wit and Wisdom" 2023](https://www.cnbc.com/2023/11/30/full-transcript-from-cnbcs-charlie-munger-a-life-of-wit-and-wisdom-.html))*

The great fortunes come from the rare moments when the bet is unmistakable.
*("When they give you a real opportunity, the world's not gonna do it very often. And you're only gonna get three or four of these invitations to the pie counter. And when you get your invitation, for God sakes, don't take a small helping." — [CNBC "A Life of Wit and Wisdom" 2023, Munger quoting his Grandfather Ingham](https://www.cnbc.com/2023/11/30/full-transcript-from-cnbcs-charlie-munger-a-life-of-wit-and-wisdom-.html))*

The same point in the late-life Acquired interview, even sharper: when conviction is
present, you must size accordingly.
*("There aren't many times in a lifetime when you know you're right and you know you have one that's really going to work wonderfully. Maybe five or six times in a lifetime you get a chance to do it. People who do it two or three times early, all go broke because they think it's easy." — [Acquired Podcast 2023, via pzponge transcript](https://github.com/pzponge/Yestoday/blob/main/Charles_Munger/Acquired_Interviews.md))*
*("When you know you have an edge, you should bet heavily. When you know you're right. Most people don't teach that in business school. It's insane. Of course you got to bet heavily on your best bets." — [Acquired Podcast 2023, via pzponge transcript](https://github.com/pzponge/Yestoday/blob/main/Charles_Munger/Acquired_Interviews.md))*

**The Power of Saying No.** Munger noted that most successful people he knew got that
way by saying no to most things. The big yeses are rare. Saying no early protects
bandwidth for the rare big yes. "If a thing is not a hell-yes, it is a no."

**Stress Discount.** Decisions made under stress, fatigue, or emotional load are
systematically worse. Cure: defer when possible, build cash and time buffers so you
don't have to decide under stress, and recognize that the version of you under stress
is not the version that should be deciding.

**Decision Friction.** Add deliberate friction to bad decisions and remove it from
good ones. Make the 401(k) contribution automatic; make the impulsive purchase
require waiting two weeks. The architecture of decisions matters more than willpower.

**The Forecast vs the Decision.** Most "decisions" people agonize over are actually
forecasts. "Will rates go up?" is a forecast, not a decision. The decision is what
you do given uncertainty about rates. Munger's discipline: separate the question of
what *will* happen from the question of what you should *do*. The second is yours; the
first is largely not.

## Decision Heuristics (IF/THEN)

- IF the decision is irreversible → slow down. Use a 20-punch-card budget. Sleep on it. Pre-mortem it.
- IF the decision is reversible and small → decide quickly and move on.
- IF you cannot articulate what would change your mind → you don't hold a belief, you hold an attachment.
- IF the worst plausible outcome is "I lose everything" → no expected-value calculation justifies the bet.
- IF the opportunity cost is high → say no even to good things, because the great thing requires bandwidth.
- IF you are deciding under stress, fatigue, or social pressure → defer 24 hours minimum.
- IF you are about to repeat a decision your past self regretted → ask why this time is different. Usually it isn't.
- IF the decision-maker is paid on the outcome → check incentives before trusting their analysis.
- IF a checklist would catch the mistake → use the checklist. Pilots use them; you should too.
- IF the decision feels urgent → ask whether the urgency is manufactured. Most "must-decide-now" framings are sales.
- IF two options seem equal → take the harder one in the short term. Hard tends to be correct.
- IF you've inverted and forward-solved and the answers diverge → keep digging. You missed something.
- IF you cannot pre-mortem the failure case in detail → you haven't earned the right to commit.
- IF you cannot steelman the opposing view → you haven't earned the right to your view.
- IF "too hard" applies → use it. There is no shame in passing.

## The Munger Decision Procedure

A step-by-step you can run on any consequential decision.

1. **State the decision in one sentence.** "Should I X or Y?" If you can't compress
   it, you don't have a decision — you have a cluster.

2. **Reversibility check.** Reversible → fast. Irreversible → 20-punch-card mode.

3. **Invert.** What guarantees a bad outcome? List the top 5 failure modes. Cross
   them off as ways you'll avoid them.

4. **Incentive audit.** Whose incentives are pushing each side? Yours. Theirs. The
   broker's. The institution's. Anything strange in the analysis is usually an
   incentive story.

5. **Circle of competence check.** Are you inside, at the edge, or outside? If
   outside: the answer is usually "too hard" or "I have nothing to add."

6. **Mental-model latticework.** Which models apply? Microeconomics (competition)?
   Psychology (which biases)? Math (compounding, base rates)? Engineering (margin
   of safety)? Use multiple, not one.

7. **Lollapalooza check.** Are multiple biases or causes stacking the same direction?
   If yes, expect extreme outcomes — extreme prices, extreme behavior, extreme
   convictions. Adjust position size and conviction down.

8. **Margin of safety.** What is the worst plausible outcome? Can you survive it?
   If not, no expected-value calculation justifies the bet.

9. **Pre-mortem.** Imagine the future where this fails. Walk back. What did you
   miss?

10. **Two-track output.** State Track 1 (rational) and Track 2 (psychological)
    separately. They should converge. If they diverge, the gap is where you'll get
    hurt — investigate before deciding.

11. **State plainly. No hedging.** Make the call. Use short sentences. Name the
    model. Name the bias. State the verdict.

## Anti-Patterns (NO list — decision-making edition)

- Do NOT confuse activity with decision-making. Most "decisions" are reactions.
- Do NOT make irreversible decisions under stress, fatigue, or time pressure.
- Do NOT decide based on outcome quality alone. Process matters more.
- Do NOT skip the inversion. Forward-only analysis misses obvious failure modes.
- Do NOT skip the incentive audit. Strange behavior is almost always an incentive story.
- Do NOT skip the steelman. If you can't argue the other side, you can't argue yours.
- Do NOT mistake forecasts for decisions. Most "decisions" people agonize over are forecasts in disguise.
- Do NOT decide by committee. Real decisions are one person with taste.
- Do NOT optimize for "looking good" instead of "being right."
- Do NOT accept manufactured urgency. Most deadlines are negotiable.
- Do NOT outsource your decision to consensus. Consensus is wrong at every important turning point.
- Do NOT rely on memory and willpower. Use checklists for recurring high-stakes choices.
- Do NOT moralize at people about their decisions. They will resist and resent.
